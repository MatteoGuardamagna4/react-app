# Training Principles

## Progressive Overload

Progressive overload is the foundational principle of all effective training programs. It states that muscles must be exposed to progressively increasing demands to continue adapting and growing. Without progressive overload, the body has no reason to get stronger or build new tissue. Methods of achieving progressive overload include: increasing the weight lifted, adding more repetitions with the same weight, performing additional sets, increasing range of motion, slowing the tempo (especially the eccentric phase), reducing rest periods, and improving exercise form. For beginners, simply adding 2.5-5kg to the bar each week on compound lifts is sufficient. Intermediate and advanced trainees need more nuanced approaches like double progression (hit the top of a rep range across all sets before increasing weight).

## Periodization

### Linear Periodization

Linear periodization involves gradually increasing intensity while decreasing volume over a training cycle of 8-16 weeks. A typical structure might begin with 4 weeks of higher volume (4 sets of 10-12 reps), progress to moderate volume and intensity (4 sets of 6-8 reps), and finish with a strength peak (5 sets of 3-5 reps). This approach works well for beginners and athletes peaking for a competition.

### Undulating Periodization

Daily undulating periodization (DUP) varies training stimuli within a single week. For example: Monday heavy (5x5), Wednesday moderate (3x10), Friday light (3x15). Research suggests DUP may produce slightly better strength and hypertrophy gains than linear models for intermediate and advanced trainees because it provides more frequent exposure to different rep ranges and training stimuli.

### Block Periodization

Block periodization dedicates 3-6 week blocks to specific training emphases. An accumulation block focuses on high volume and moderate intensity. An intensification block reduces volume and increases loads. A realization block involves the heaviest weights with minimal volume. This approach is popular with competitive powerlifters and athletes with specific performance dates.

## Training Splits

### Full Body Training

Full body training hits every major muscle group in each session, typically 3 times per week. This is ideal for beginners because each muscle gets trained frequently, which accelerates motor learning and strength gains. Example schedule: Monday, Wednesday, Friday with rest days between. Each session includes a squat pattern, a hip hinge, a horizontal push, a horizontal pull, and a vertical push or pull.

### Upper/Lower Split

The upper-lower split divides training into upper body and lower body days, typically 4 sessions per week. This allows more volume per muscle group than full body while maintaining good training frequency. Example: Monday upper, Tuesday lower, Thursday upper, Friday lower. This split suits intermediate lifters who have outgrown full body programs.

### Push/Pull/Legs

Push-pull-legs (PPL) organizes training by movement pattern: push days (chest, shoulders, triceps), pull days (back, biceps, rear delts), and leg days (quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves). Running PPL twice per week (6 sessions) provides excellent volume and frequency for each muscle group. This split is popular with intermediate to advanced lifters focused on muscle hypertrophy.

## Training Volume

Volume refers to the total amount of work performed, typically measured as the number of hard sets per muscle group per week. Research-supported volume landmarks include: Minimum Effective Volume (MEV) of approximately 6-8 sets per muscle group per week to maintain size, Maximum Recoverable Volume (MRV) of roughly 15-25 sets depending on the muscle group and the individual, and a Maximum Adaptive Volume (MAV) sweet spot between these extremes where most growth occurs. Start at MEV and gradually increase volume over a training block until you approach your MRV, then deload.

## Training Frequency

Training frequency refers to how often each muscle group is trained per week. Current evidence supports training each muscle group 2-3 times per week for optimal hypertrophy, as this distributes volume across more sessions and maintains a more consistent muscle protein synthesis response. Training a muscle once per week can still produce results if weekly volume is adequate, but dividing the same volume across multiple sessions is generally superior.

## Autoregulation with RPE

Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE) on a 1-10 scale allows you to adjust training intensity based on daily readiness. An RPE of 10 means no more reps could have been performed (absolute failure). RPE 8 means 2 reps were left in reserve. Most training should be conducted at RPE 7-9, leaving 1-3 reps in reserve. This approach accounts for day-to-day variations in sleep, stress, nutrition, and recovery. Training to absolute failure on every set increases fatigue disproportionately relative to the stimulus gained and raises injury risk.
